About

The Australian Government have recently passed a bill that will allow them to force your Internet Service Provider (ISP) to block certain websites so that you can't access them. This is kind of like geo-blocking, except instead of a website blocking you because you live in a certain country, it's the other way around, and the country is blocking you from accessing that website.

This site has been created to help people take control of what they can and can't view on the Internet.
I will try and explain the ideas in as basic a sense as I can, however I am by no means an expert on these topics, I have simply done some research into options for myself, and in doing that, thought I could share what I have learnt so that other people could also do the same, if they chose to.

I'm going to discuss the use of Virtual Private Networks (VPN) as it is regarded as the best in terms of speed and privacy, however this isn't the only option to take control of your Internet surfing activities.

Note: Using a VPN will also prevent the ISP from retaining meta-data about you, which they are now also required to do, by law.

VPN

A VPN is a way for you to communicate with websites on the Internet whilst being completely anonymous, secure and private, ensuring websites can't track you, hackers can't steal your information, and ISP's can't deny you access to any websites.

How will the Government's site blocking work?

The Government's site blocking will likely work by telling ISP's (or mailmen, in our video example) to block any requests to a certain URL (address). When the ISP see's that you're requesting a URL/address of a website that is blocked, they won't send the request to view to the server hosting that web page. Instead, they will display a page to you indicating that the website is blocked. By using a VPN, or by using Vicky, the ISP/mailman has no idea that you're trying to access a blocked URL/address, because they only see the request to go to the VPN/Vicky.

VPN Features

What features should you look for when choosing a VPN provider? The following list should be considered when selecting a VPN provider that is right for you

Logs

If the VPN provider keeps logs, then that's effectively keeping a record of all the sites that you visit. Ideally, you want a provider that doesn't keep logs

Protocol

The protocol refers to the algorithm used to encrypt your data. I would suggest going with a VPN provider that supports OpenVPN

Bandwidth

In a nutshell, bandwidth is the amount of data you're allowed to download. Everything you do on the web is downloading data, so you will want to look for a provider that doesn't limit this

VPN Client

Some VPN providers have operating specific clients that you can download and use to start the VPN running, which makes it much easier than having to play with IP settings in a router

Payment System

Some VPN providers may only accept certain forms of payment. They might be forms that you're not comfortable with, so it's something you'll need to consider

Exit Locations

Ensure the VPN provider has multiple exit locations around the world, this will ensure you have the best chance of avoiding Internet censorship around the world

Mobile App

Finding a VPN that provides mobile apps so you can be safe and secure whilst using a mobile device is also something that you might want to consider

Cost

To be able to get all of these features, you're likely going to have to pay for a subscription to a VPN provider. This will typically be a montly sum

Simultaneous Connections

Being able to connect to your VPN provider through multiple devices at once is also something that you may want to think about. For example, you may want to connect your mobile, your desktop computer, and your laptop and tablet for on the go, safe Internet surfing

Feedback

Finally, if you do some Googling for the VPN providers name, you should be able to find user feedback about them. Their website will only have positive reviews, but a simple Google search will find those negative ones, if any
